MOSFET Performance

To design an amplifier, the properties of the transistor must be understood. MOFETs are nonlinear 4-terminal devices that allow for many basic functions in most circuits to be possible. MOSFET stands for metal oxide semiconductor field effect transistor, describing how the body of the device is made and functions. This device has 4 terminals, the gate, source, drain and body. These terminals can be powered in certain ways to achieve different results. Generally the body will be tied to the source of the MOSFET or grounded, as the body has to be tied to the lowest potential of the circuit to maintain the polarity of the MOSFET and prevent the body effect. MOSFETS can make use of free electrons or electron “holes” to create a channel that allows electrons to flow from the source to the drain, when under the influence of a strong enough field. This is done by “doping” the substrate with either holes or electrons which determines if the transistor is an n-MOS or p-MOS.

MOSFETs can be used to replace many components as they can take up considerably less area which can be very expensive on a chip. For example, connecting the gate and the drain of a MOSFET (called a gated diode) can act as an active resistor, which can have a variable resistance and takes up much less area than a resistor. The experiments in this lab study how changing the lengths of the n-mos and p-mos transistors affect different parameters and their functions. The parameters include:
  • Vt: Threshold voltage
  • λ: Channel length modulation
  • K: Transconductance parameter
  • GmrDS:Intrinsic Gain

Operating Principle

The MOSFET can function in the three following modes: cutoff, linear and saturation. First it is important to discuss important parameters of the MOSFET. Vt is the threshold voltage determined by the design and technology of the MOSFET. Kn' is the transconductance parameter of the transistor, dependent on the electron mobility, width/length ratio, and gate oxide capacitance. λ is the channel length modulation, impacting the stability of the current for a variable VGS, where a smaller CLM is desired and results in a flatter ID curve in the saturation region.

Cutoff region is when the MOSFET is “off”, it is not conducting and has no channel for electron flow. When the voltage across the gate and source is less than the threshold voltage. The transistor is in the linear region when VGS is greater than VT, but the voltage difference between the drain and source is less than the difference between VGS and VT. In this region, the IDS increases linearly with VDS hence the name. This region is undesirable for current source applications due to the slope of the IDS curve. The IDS in this region increases with VDS as the channel between the drain and the source is forming, and increasing in length in the MOSFET. When VDS is greater than VGS-VT the transistor is in the saturation region, meaning VD must be higher than VG-VT. This is when the channel for electron flow is fully formed, and the same amount of current is flowing with an increasing VDS, it can be said the channel is fully saturated. From the IV characteristics the current in the drain can be modeled with the following equation:

$$i_D = \frac{1}{2}k'_n\left(\frac{W}{L}\right)(v_{GS} - V_{tn})^2 (1 + \lambda v_{DS})$$
